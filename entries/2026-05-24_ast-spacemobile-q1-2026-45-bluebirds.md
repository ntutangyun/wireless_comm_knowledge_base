## Summary (EN)

Via Satellite's May 12 2026 report (Rachel Jewett) on AST SpaceMobile's Q1 2026 earnings call delivers four datapoints worth tracking. (1) **The 45-satellite year-end target is reaffirmed** — CEO Abel Avellan confirmed it on the call despite an April Blue Origin New Glenn mishap that deployed a satellite into the wrong orbit. The company quantifies the Blue Origin impact in its 10-Q as **$155M–$160M of Q2 2026 asset write-offs**; insurance covers portions of both satellite and launch expense, with claims filed. The 33-satellite factory inventory is the cushion — CSO Scott Wisniewski stated 33 BlueBirds are in advanced stages of production. (2) **Next launch concrete**: SpaceX Falcon 9 mid-June, carrying BlueBirds 8, 9, 10. (3) **Q1 financials**: **$14.7M revenue, up 20× YoY from $718k**, driven by commercial gateway deliveries + US government milestones; sequential revenue declined ~$40M on customer-deployment / government-contract completion timing. (4) **Constellation-sizing claim**: CFO Andrew Johnson on the call — "**~45–60 BlueBirds enables continuous SpaceMobile service across US, Europe, Japan, and other strategic markets**", with sufficient launch capacity contracted across SpaceX + Blue Origin + ULA.

Two regulatory/technical milestones from the May 11 Q1 release deserve emphasis: **FCC commercial SpaceMobile Service authorization in the US was granted** (the missing piece for direct-to-device-from-space to invoiced US consumers, beyond the partner-carrier overlay model), and **the in-orbit Block 1 BlueBird achieved 98.9 Mbps peak data speeds over international waters** — a measurement on an actual satellite, not a simulation. AST also reports three new US Government awards since March 2026 via prime contractors.

The bigger picture: AST SpaceMobile is now demonstrably past the technical-feasibility phase (FCC commercial, 98.9 Mbps measured, multi-launch-provider redundancy), but still has a brittle scaling story — the Blue Origin Q2 write-off shows how a single launch failure absorbs $150M+ of the deployment budget. The 33-satellite factory inventory is what keeps the deployment-cadence story intact through 2026.

## Why it matters / what's new (EN)

For the satellite-direct-to-device thread in the KB, this is the most concrete operator-side data point of 2026 to date. Three angles stand out. First, **the 45–60 BlueBird service threshold is a hard number** — it gives the rest of the industry a benchmark for what continuous coverage costs in a phased-array LEO architecture. Second, **the FCC commercial authorization closes a US regulatory gap** that AST has been working since 2023 — combined with the 98.9 Mbps real-link measurement, this moves AST from "trial deployment" to "commercial service" in the US specifically. Third, **the Blue Origin write-off ($155M–$160M for one wrong-orbit deployment)** quantifies the launch-failure tail risk for LEO direct-to-device — a useful data point for any constellation-economics modelling.
