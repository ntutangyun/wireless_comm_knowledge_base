## Summary (EN)
At Computex 2026 (early June), Asus unveiled the ROG Rapture GT-BN98 Pro, billed as the world's first gaming router built on Wi-Fi 8 (IEEE 802.11bn). The unit claims aggregate speeds up to 30 Gbps and, per Asus, up to a 2× real-world throughput uplift over Wi-Fi 7 in its target scenarios. The board carries eight antennas with an active-cooling system and a seven-port wired backbone: one gigabit LAN, three 2.5 GbE LAN, one 2.5 GbE LAN/WAN, one 10 GbE LAN/WAN, and a dedicated 10 GbE "gaming port" that auto-prioritises latency-sensitive traffic. It won a Computex Best Choice award.

Notably, Asus frames the product around the 802.11bn reliability story rather than peak PHY rate: the marketing leans on multi-AP coordination (MAPC), intelligent path optimisation, and dynamic bandwidth management — the Ultra-High-Reliability (UHR) themes that define the draft amendment. Because 802.11bn is not yet ratified (final ratification projected for 2028), this is pre-standard silicon; Asus gave no firm ship date, with industry estimates pointing to Q4 2026 / Q1 2027.

## Why it matters / what's new (EN)
This is a product unveil, not a new mechanism — hence novelty 1. Its value is as a deployment-timeline datapoint: it marks the first *named, awarded* consumer Wi-Fi 8 router, moving the bin past roadmap teases (cf. the TP-Link Archer 8 roadmap, 2026-05-30) to a concrete, demonstrated SKU. It also signals that vendors are commercialising MAPC — the multi-AP coordination work tracked in the `mapc-cosr` bin — as a consumer-facing feature ahead of IEEE ratification, which is worth watching for how pre-standard MAPC implementations diverge from the eventual 802.11bn text.
