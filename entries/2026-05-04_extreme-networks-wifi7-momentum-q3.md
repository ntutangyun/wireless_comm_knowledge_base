## Summary (EN)

Network World's 1-May-2026 read of Extreme Networks' Q3 FY26 results (quarter ending 31 March 2026) is one of the cleanest public data points so far on enterprise Wi-Fi 7 share-of-shipment. Extreme reported **Wi-Fi 7 = 37% of wireless unit shipments**, up from 27% the prior quarter, and **nearly half of wireless bookings** came from Wi-Fi 7 SKUs. CEO Ed Meyercord framed Wi-Fi 7 not as a throughput win but as "a meaningful step up... from a quality and reliability perspective" — i.e. the deterministic-features story (MLO, MU-OFDMA, R-TWT) is what is selling to enterprise IT, consistent with the same-quarter Cisco thesis (`cisco-wifi7-breakout-year-2026`).

Two non-WLAN factoids from the call are worth carrying because they shape the *supply* side of Wi-Fi 7 deployment. First, Extreme has secured DDR memory supply "through fiscal 2027 and then the first quarter of fiscal 2028" — explicitly a competitive-advantage move while DDR remains constrained for AP / switch silicon. Second, the company points to fabric-driven deployment speed (a cited customer migration "took us six hours with Cisco, took only six minutes with Extreme") and end-to-end L2 segmentation across wired / wireless / SD-WAN as differentiators. Five consecutive quarters of double-digit growth, Q3 sales of $316.9M (+11% YoY) — i.e. the financial print is consistent with the share-shift narrative rather than just hopeful guidance.

For the KB this is the second concrete enterprise vendor data point (alongside Cisco's qualitative thesis) showing the Wi-Fi 7 transition is now measurable in shipped-units, not just SKUs-on-shelf.

## Why it matters / what's new (EN)

The KB has multiple Wi-Fi 7 / Wi-Fi 8 announcement entries but very few that quantify *adoption*. Extreme's 27% → 37% quarter-over-quarter shift is the clearest single number we have for the rate of enterprise Wi-Fi 7 transition, and it lines up with `realtek-wifi7-revenue-surge-q1-2026` (silicon-side demand pull) and `cisco-wifi7-breakout-year-2026` (Cisco's qualitative thesis). The DDR-supply detail is also a useful piece of context for *why* not every vendor is shipping at the same rate — a Wi-Fi 7 AP needs more on-board RAM than a Wi-Fi 6 AP for MLO state and 4K-QAM PHY pipelines, and DDR availability is currently a gating factor on platform refreshes. None of this is on the standards side, but it grounds the standards work in deliverable hardware.
